Max Payne 3, Correctly Understood

In my review of Max Payne 3, I suggested that the game was lacking the soul that the two preceding games had. I stand by this: in its theme, its tone, its plot—really in terms of all that distinguishes Max Payne from other shooters, including in large part the combat—it just does not fit.

But it’s not soulless, just mislabelled. About halfway through the game, I had a sudden realisation and understanding. I saw the game in a new light, and seen this way it all fits together; it all works.

Max Payne 3 is a Die Hard game.

Max had buried his grief like bullets in a thousand mobster corpses, and as Mona Sax died had found peace with both her death and the deaths of his wife and child. “I had a dream of my wife. She was dead. But it was all right.” are the final words of Max Payne 2—wherever Max is going from there, it isn’t to the pills and the bottle, as Max Payne 3 suggests.

But John McClane, former New York cop, whose perennial relationship problems with his wife bookend the films—McClane had already turned to drink…
