Escherisch

For Mapcore’s cube challenge, I built a Half-Life 2 Episode Two map. It had to be constrained to 1024x1024x1024 units.

Typically when confronted with an arbitrary constraint, I try to find a way to work around it. For example, a TF2maps mini-contest challenged us to build a control point in 2048x2048 units (no height limit), but without a 3D skybox. So I built the point, and a huge window out of which you could see stars, and a large spinning Earth. It looked like the Earth was in a 3D skybox, but in fact it was a complicated fake involving texture proxies–and was a great learning exercise for me; texture proxies are surprisingly powerful in Source.

My original idea for the mapcore challenge was to make the map appear to be larger than 1024^3. For example, the starting room was going to be the interior of a room that appeared to be about 2048 units long. You would be able to walk around the entire room, and thus apparently be in a larger level. To do this (and similar tricks), I was going to segment the vertical space into stacked slices, each 128 or 256 units high, and duplicated the room geometry into two of these slices; each having one half of the room within the 1024^3 cube. And I’d use a trigger_teleport to transport the player between each half, thus making it appear to be a single continuous space.

This was certainly doable, but would have been a pretty boring space. I didn’t really have any clear idea what I would do with the space beyond this illusion. So casting around for inspiration, I ran across Escher’s Ascending and Descending again. This time I noticed not the endless staircase illusion, but the surrounding architecture: low sloped roofs, flat roofs, arches, stairways. I quickly got the vision of a small village, with small passageways, stairways onto roofs and into basements, with teleports to make physically impossible connections between them. So that is what I built.

Download the map here. Put it in your Steam folder, in the steamapps/yourusername/half-life 2 episode two/ep2/maps subfolder, then run Episode 2 and launch the map by typing map escherisch in the console.

There are two impossible connections between the edges of the map at ground level. The top of the tower can be reached, but the stairs inside it will not take you there, though they are not a dead end. And one passageway will lead you to the bridge visible in the background of the picture above. The bridge itself is outside the 1024^3 playable area, but the player is not; the map itself is used as its own 3D skybox at a 1:1 scale to make this illusion.

Two key factors influence the look of this map. I found early on that I was unable to get the precalculated radiosity lightmaps to match in both areas around a teleport; this led to a noticeable shift in shadows or colours while teleporting, breaking the illusion. So I went with a fullbright map–or rather, a map with only UnlitGeneric textures. So I detailed the map with 11 shades of grey, applying them manually to provide the appearance of flat lighting, while maintaining contrast between the visible shapes. That turned out to be quite an interesting challenge, and very helpful in emphasizing the silhouettes of the geometry that the player encounters.

In a last minute change, I added colour correction, shading all light grey tones with a very faint, barely noticeable sepia, and all mid to dark grey tones with a dull red. White and black remained untinted; I think the result ended up much more attractive than the plain greys. However, I submitted the map in a rush, as I had to leave early the next day, and forgot to include the colour correction table. So while my screenshots showed the red tinting, those trying out the map would unfortunately only see it in greyscale. Oh well.
